Key Legal Milestones After WWII

1945 – Nuremberg Trials Begin

Allied powers initiate the first major international war crimes tribunal, prosecuting top Nazi leaders for crimes against humanity.

Time: 20.11.1945 - 01.10.1946

Location: The Nuremberg Palace of Justice, Nuremberg

Defendants: 24

Significance: The first time in history that individuals rather than just states—were held accountable for crimes against peace, war crimes, and crimes against humanity. Also laid the legal foundation of these crimes that remain central to international law today.

1950 – Formation of the GDR (East Germany) Justice System

East Germany establishes its own socialist legal system with ideological goals, including trials of Nazi collaborators as part of denazification.

1958 – West German Central Office for Investigating Nazi Crimes Established

West Germany creates a dedicated institution in Ludwigsburg to pursue remaining Nazi crimes, reflecting Cold War pressure and shifting memory politics.

1963 – Frankfurt Auschwitz Trial Begin

The first major post-Nuremberg trial in West Germany to confront Auschwitz crimes. It marks a cultural turning point in public memory.

Time: 20.12.1963 - 19.08.1965

Location: Bürgerhaus at Frankenallee, Frankfurt am Main-Gallus

Defendants: 22

Significance: The first major domestic trial in the FRG (West Germany), revealing ordinary Germans’ involvement in mass murder. Symbolized a shift from silence and denial to public accountability.

1966 – Horst Fischer Trial Begin

A high-profile GDR trial of an SS doctor, used as a political counterpoint to West German justice. Fischer is sentenced to death.

Time: 10.03.1966 - 25.03.1966

Location: Supreme Court of the GDR, Berlin

Defendants: 1

Significance: GDR authorities used the trial to claim that true justice for Nazi crimes could only be achieved under socialism, while painting West Germany as lenient and complicit in rehabilitating former Nazis.

1980s–1990s – Renewed Interest in Nazi Trials

Cold War ends and reunification prompts archival access and a new wave of historical reckoning, including trials of lower-level participants.
