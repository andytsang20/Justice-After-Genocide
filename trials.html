Introduction

This page examines two significant trials from post-war Germany: the Frankfurt Auschwitz Trial and the Horst Fischer Trial. These trials were selected because they clearly demonstrate how the prosecution of Nazi war criminals was influenced by Cold War politics, highlighting stark differences in legal frameworks, judicial procedures, and ideological approaches between West and East Germany.

Frankfurt Auschwitz Trial (1963–1965)

The Frankfurt Auschwitz Trial was a landmark event held in West Germany, marking the prosecution of 22 former SS personnel responsible for atrocities at Auschwitz. This trial was pivotal in confronting Germany's Nazi past, providing detailed testimony that significantly influenced public awareness and understanding of the Holocaust.

Date of Prosecution 20.12.1963 - 19.08.1965(20Months)
Number of Judges3
Number of Defendants22
Accused CrimeCrimes against humanity, murder
Legal BasisGerman Criminal Code
Codes Used in ProsecutionStGB (German Penal Code)
Verdict 6 life sentences, 11 imprisonments of 3–14 years, and 5 acquitted

Horst Fischer Trial (1965, East Germany)

The Horst Fischer Trial was held in East Germany, prosecuting Horst Fischer, an SS doctor accused of war crimes at Auschwitz. Fischer was sentenced to death and executed in 1967. This trial served as a political statement by the GDR, emphasizing its antifascist stance and distinguishing its judicial approach from West Germany.

Date of Prosecution10.03.1966 - 25.03.1966(15Days)
Number of Judges3
Number of Defendants1
Accused CrimeWar crimes, crimes against humanity
Legal BasisGDR Criminal Code
Codes Used in ProsecutionGDR Penal Code
VerdictDeath sentence, executed in Leipzig on 08.07.1966

Comparison of the Trials

The Frankfurt Auschwitz Trial and the Horst Fischer Trial represent different approaches to prosecuting Nazi war criminals, influenced by Cold War dynamics. West Germany emphasized procedural legality and detailed evidentiary proceedings, aiming at broader public awareness and historical accountability. East Germany used the trial to project a political message emphasizing antifascist legitimacy and swift justice. These contrasting trials illustrate how judicial proceedings were influenced by ideological differences and geopolitical pressures of the Cold War era.
